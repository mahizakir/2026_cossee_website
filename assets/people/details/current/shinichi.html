Shinichi is a Professor of Evolutionary Biology & Synthesis at the University of Alberta. He now leads COSSEE through the Canada Excellence Research Chair program in Open Science and Synthesis in Ecology and Evolution.

I was born in Nagano and brought up in Saitama, Japan. After turning 21, I went to New Zealand to see penguins. I did BSc (Hons) at the University of Waikato, New Zealand (2003) and PhD at the University of Sheffield, United Kingdom (2007). After a brief postdoc period, I got a job as lecturer at the University of Otago (2008). In 2015, I moved to UNSW, Sydney and from 2024, I am the Canada Excellence Research Chair (CERC) in Open Science and Synthesis in Ecology and Evolution at the University of Alberta. I love sleeping a lot during weekends and watching Japanese cartoons.

Research Interests

My research interests are very diverse but usually fall into three categories: 1) Research Synthesis, 2) Meta-science and 3) Methodological Development. This mostly means statistical methods, but it also includes broader methodological work.

My main model organisms have primarily been birds, especially sparrows, dunnocks and penguins, but I have also worked with earthworms, amphipods, frogs and fish. Between 2015 and 2020, our main experimental model was zebrafish, and between 2021 and 2023 our main system was fruit flies. After COVID, I decided to focus mainly on research synthesis, including meta-analyses, big-data analyses, and meta-science.

Past Work / Education

I used to study too hard during my days in Japan and I burnt out and wanted to escape. Then, New Zealand seemed to be a great place; they have penguins. I was very fortunate to do my BSc degree with Honours in Psychology and Biology at the University of Waikato under the supervision of Prof Joe Waas and Prof Mary Foster. Joe basically taught me how to write in English; before that I was only able to write in Japanese.

During my honours time, I worked on kin recognition in little penguins on Tiritiri Matagi Island, tested a hormonal assay with Adelie penguins in Antarctica, and studied memory in chickens. I then moved to the UK for a PhD at the University of Sheffield under Prof Ben Hatchwell and Prof Terry Burke, where I studied parental care in relation to male ornamentation in house sparrows on Lundy Island. I continued working with Lundy sparrows as a postdoc, then returned to New Zealand in 2008. After seven years at the University of Otago, I moved to the Evolution & Ecology Research Centre and the School of Biological, Earth and Environmental Sciences at UNSW Sydney, where I stayed until 2024. I now head COSSEE at the University of Alberta.

Current Research / Work

At COSSEE I lead a Canada Excellence Research Chair program on Open Science and Synthesis in Ecology and Evolution. The central aim is simple but ambitious: to make ecological and evolutionary research more trustworthy, efficient, and reusable by combining research synthesis, meta-science, open workflows, and new statistical and AI tools.

Our work is organised around four interconnected strands:

  • Mapping and living syntheses: We are building systematic, continuously updated overviews of evolutionary ecology using research weaving, systematic maps, bibliometrics, and living reviews and meta-analyses. These projects aim to show where evidence is dense, where gaps remain, and how ideas and researchers are connected.
  • Quantifying and validating replicability: Ecology and evolution rarely allow simple direct replications, but they still need reliable, testable evidence. We are assessing the replicability of key empirical studies and meta-analyses, creating a reliability map of the field, and testing those predictions through DEAR workshops that re-run and update influential syntheses.
  • Building open, AI-assisted infrastructure: With computer and data scientists, we are developing semi-automated pipelines for screening, classifying, and extracting data from the literature; creating research-weaving tools and dashboards; building EcoEvo-specific staged pre-registration infrastructure; and co-creating dynamic community guidelines that extend work such as PRISMA-EcoEvo.
  • New variance-aware methods and data recycling: My group works on methods that go beyond mean differences by comparing variance, covariance, skewness, and kurtosis; using individual-participant-data meta-analysis and large databases; and developing robust meta-meta-analysis methods to synthesise across many existing meta-analyses.

This variance- and distribution-aware perspective underpins much of COSSEE's work, linking meta-science, open-science practice, and evolutionary and ecological theory.
